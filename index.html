Meet Jackie

From Refugee
to Ward 1's
Loudest Voice.

Jackie Reyes Yanes arrived in Washington, DC in 1990 — at just 12 years old, escaping civil war in El Salvador, landing in Mount Pleasant with nothing. She became a young mother. She survived homelessness. She found her footing at the Latin American Youth Center and let that experience set the direction of her life.

Everything Jackie fights for, she has lived. Affordable housing isn't a policy paper to her — it's the story of her father buying a home on a dishwasher's wages. Universal childcare isn't a campaign promise — it's what she needed and didn't have.

Government Track Record

She Doesn't Just Talk.
She Delivers.

Before running for Council, Jackie built a career in DC government getting things done for real people — securing millions in funding, protecting families from displacement, and opening doors for immigrants and young people who had none. This is what she'll bring to Ward 1.

01
$400K → $3.5M
Immigrant Justice Grant

Jackie supported the creation of DC's Immigrant Justice Legal Services Grant — providing free immigration legal representation to DC's immigrant community. She helped grow it from an initial $400,000 into a $3.5 million coalition fund, giving thousands of DC immigrants access to attorneys who can fight for their right to stay.

02
Families Kept Home
Monsignor Romero Apartments — Saved After Fire

When fire devastated the Monsignor Romero Apartments, residents faced the very real threat of permanent displacement. Jackie stepped in, fought for the residents, and helped them form a housing cooperative — giving the families legal ownership and control of their own building so they could continue living in their community, not lose it.

03
$12 Million+
Emergency Rental Relief for DC Residents

During the housing crisis, Jackie helped thousands of DC residents navigate the city's emergency rental assistance programs — directly facilitating more than $12 million in financial support for families behind on rent, preventing evictions and keeping people housed when they had nowhere else to turn.

04
Language Access Expanded
City Services in Your Language

Jackie expanded language access across DC's constituent services agencies — specifically within the Mayor's Office of Community Affairs — ensuring that residents who speak Spanish and other languages could access the same city services and receive the same quality of help as English-speaking residents. No one left behind because of language.

05
Youth Employment Created
Summer Youth Employment Program for Immigrants

Jackie created DC's Summer Youth Employment Program for recently arrived immigrant youth — opening the door for young newcomers to earn income, build work experience, and participate in the same opportunities as their DC peers, giving them a real foothold in their new city from day one.

06
$43 Million+
Emergency Immigrant Support Fund

When the federal relief package left DC's immigrant community behind, Jackie helped create the DC Emergency Immigrant Support Fund — a first-of-its-kind safety net providing direct financial support to immigrants excluded from federal benefits. The fund delivered more than $43 million to thousands of DC immigrant families who had nowhere else to turn.

The Platform

One plan.
Five achievable
promises.
Zero excuses.

These aren't talking points drafted by a consultant. They're commitments built from 30 years of conversations in Adams Morgan, Columbia Heights, Mount Pleasant, Petworth, and Park View. Jackie will deliver them starting Day 1.

01

Keep Ward 1 Affordable for Everyone.

Too many of our neighbors — the teachers, nurses, restaurant workers, and artists who make Ward 1 who it is — are being pushed out. Jackie's father bought their home in the 1990s working as a dishwasher. That door must stay open for the next generation.

"My father bought this home on a dishwasher's wages. That dream should still be possible. I'll fight every single day to make sure it is."

— Jackie Reyes Yanes

  • Expand Homeownership via HPAP

    Raise DC's Home Purchase Assistance Program fund to $30 million and lift the individual income limit to $150,000 — opening the door for teachers, nurses, first responders, and working professionals who earn too much to qualify but too little to buy.

  • Strengthen Rent Control

    Close the loopholes landlords use to skip DC's rent stabilization rules. No more renovictions. No more bad-faith vacancy filings. Your rent is your rent.

  • Tenant Right of First Refusal

    When a building sells, its tenants get the first offer — at fair market value. No more being blindsided when a new owner shows up.

  • Anti-Displacement Task Force — With Real Tools

    Four concrete actions: (1) an Inspection Request Team responding within 72 hours; (2) a Vacant Property Partnership Program converting empty buildings into affordable units; (3) expedited permitting so housing gets built instead of stuck; (4) block-by-block displacement risk tracking with direct intervention for families at risk.

  • More Affordable Units, Now

    All new Ward 1 developments above 10 units must include at least 20% affordable at 60% AMI or below. No waivers. No buyouts.

02

No Parent Chooses Between Career and Caregiving.

Childcare in DC costs over $2,500 a month — more than most mortgages. Ward 1 families are making impossible choices every single day. Jackie will end that, because a city that doesn't invest in its youngest residents has given up on its future.

"Childcare shouldn't cost $2,500 a month. You're working hard for your family. This city should work just as hard for you."

— Jackie Reyes Yanes

  • Universal Pre-K 3 and Pre-K 4

    Free, universal Pre-K 3 and Pre-K 4 for every Ward 1 child — regardless of family income. No waitlists. No exceptions. Every 3- and 4-year-old gets a high-quality seat before kindergarten.

  • Fair Pay for Early Childhood Educators

    Salary supplements bringing all early childhood educators to at least $22/hour — with benefits and professional development. The people shaping our children's first years deserve professional pay, not poverty wages.

  • Build New Childcare Facilities — City & Private Spaces

    Use city-owned vacant properties and underutilized privately-owned spaces — church halls, community centers, commercial ground floors — to build and renovate childcare facilities across Ward 1. More seats, lower costs, closer to home.

  • Subsidized Infant & Toddler Care

    Expand subsidy eligibility to families earning up to 150% AMI — covering working Ward 1 families who earn too much to qualify but too little to pay $2,500 a month.

03

Small Business Activation & El Barrio Latino Corridor.

Mt. Pleasant and Columbia Heights are the cultural heartbeat of DC's Latino community. Rising commercial rents and lack of capital are forcing out the bodegas, taquerías, and family shops that define our barrio. Jackie's Small Business Activation Program puts real money — up to $50,000 in direct grants — behind every Ward 1 business that needs it.

"Our barrio is not just a neighborhood — it's a living culture. If we lose the businesses, we lose the soul. I'll make sure that doesn't happen."

— Jackie Reyes Yanes

  • Small Business Activation Grants — $5,000 to $50,000

    A dedicated Ward 1 grant fund — $5,000 to $50,000 — for any Ward 1 small business, for equipment, renovations, inventory, hiring, and expansion. No bureaucratic maze. Real capital, fast decisions, in English and Spanish.

  • El Barrio Latino Cultural Corridor — Mt. Pleasant & Columbia Heights

    Officially designate and protect the El Barrio Latino Cultural and Commercial Corridor along Mt. Pleasant St NW and 14th St NW in Columbia Heights — storefront preservation incentives, bilingual signage, and cultural identity standards for new commercial tenants. The soul of our barrio, protected by law.

  • Commercial Rent Stabilization Program

    Cap annual rent increases for ground-floor small businesses at no more than 5% per year. Longtime corridor anchors stay put instead of being priced out by speculative landlords.

  • Festival Activation — Culture on Every Corner

    Year-round neighborhood activations — Día de los Muertos, Feria de las Flores, Latin Jazz on the Plaza, night markets on Mt. Pleasant St. Culture drives foot traffic. Foot traffic keeps businesses alive.

  • One-Stop Business Support Desk

    A bilingual navigator at the Neighborhood Solution Center cutting business opening time from 6+ months to 60 days — permits, licenses, inspections, and grant applications all in one place.

04

Safe Streets Start with Community, Not Just Cops.

Real safety is kids outside after dark. Seniors at the bus stop without fear. Neighbors who know each other's names. Jackie's approach: smart infrastructure, activated public spaces, and community-driven programs that address root causes — not just symptoms.

"A well-lit block with neighbors who look out for each other is safer than any patrol car. We reclaim our streets by investing in them — and in each other."

— Jackie Reyes Yanes

  • Light Every Block in Ward 1

    Full audit and upgrade of every street light in Ward 1 — prioritizing alleys, transit stops, and park paths most frequently reported as unsafe. Bright streets are safe streets. Year 1 commitment.

  • 200 New Trees

    Plant 200 trees along Ward 1's most-traveled corridors. Greenery slows traffic, reduces stress, creates natural gathering spots, and shows this community invests in itself.

  • Activate Public Spaces

    Year-round programming in Ward 1 parks and plazas — outdoor movies, weekend markets, sports leagues, senior fitness classes. Public spaces belong to the community, not to fear.

  • Ward 1 Violence Interruption Program

    Expand DC's Cure the Streets model into every Ward 1 neighborhood — credible messengers from our own community de-escalating conflict before it becomes violence.

  • Youth Employment = Community Safety

    Guaranteed summer and after-school jobs for every Ward 1 young person who wants one. A teen with a job, a paycheck, and a mentor is the most effective crime prevention strategy we have.

05

The Neighborhood Solution Center — Your Ward, Served.

The NSC isn't a hotline or a website. It's a ward-based hub where seniors and young people work side by side as paid Community Ambassadors — answering phones, walking Ward 1 streets, delivering direct support to residents. Real people. Real help.

"I want a grandmother from Petworth and a 19-year-old from Columbia Heights both working for their community — and both getting a paycheck for it. That's the NSC."

— Jackie Reyes Yanes

  • Community Ambassador Corps

    20 part-time paid Ambassadors — 10 seniors (55+) and 10 young people (18–24). They staff the phone line, walk Ward 1 corridors, and connect residents to city services. Starting wage: $18/hour.

  • Street-Level Support — On Your Block

    Ambassadors walk assigned Ward 1 neighborhoods weekly — checking on elderly residents, flagging broken lights, potholes, and trash, distributing resources in English and Spanish.

  • Constituent Hotline — 72-Hour Response Guarantee

    Call 202-724-8181. A real person answers who knows your neighborhood. Every case opened, tracked, resolved. Monthly public dashboards track resolution rates. No runaround.

  • Intergenerational Mentorship Built In

    Seniors and youth Ambassadors are paired together — real mentorship happening through the work itself. Older residents bring neighborhood history; young people bring energy and tech.

  • Monthly Neighborhood Town Halls

    In-person meetings in Adams Morgan, Columbia Heights, Mount Pleasant, Petworth, and Park View every month — in English and Spanish. Jackie shows up. No exceptions.
